# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

**Primary: prospective lay learners.** Adult Reformed and Presbyterian Christians, plus curious newcomers, weighing whether to enroll in a course. Most have no formal theological training. They arrive asking whether the Academy is serious, trustworthy, and approachable for someone like them, then want to see what is offered (courses, terms, cost), judge the faculty, and decide whether to express interest or book an intro call. They are usually on a phone (~375px), often browsing in the evening, and comparing this against other formation options or simply self-study.

**Secondary:** returning learners re-enrolling for the next term, and donors plus the funding Presbytery of Cincinnati assessing the school's legitimacy and stewardship.

## Product Purpose

The Presbyterian Academy is a Reformed lay-formation school, founded in 2026 and funded by the Presbytery of Cincinnati. This site is the school's public front door. Its job is to move an interested visitor from "what is this?" to enrolled: browse the course catalog, meet credentialed faculty, understand pricing and need-based scholarships, then convert through an express-interest form and a Calendly intro call. Success looks like qualified express-interest submissions and intro calls booked, with the catalog and faculty pages carrying the credibility. It is not a church website, and not a logged-in learning platform; the teaching happens in in-person cohorts.

## Brand Personality

Scholarly, plain-spoken, inviting. The voice is warm and unpretentious, like a knowledgeable friend who saved you a seat: it takes the material, and God, seriously without taking itself too seriously. It shows the thinking instead of listing credentials, says things plainly (especially about money), and stops once the point is made. The whole thing should read modern but long-standing and credible, closer to a serious reading room than a church foyer. Detailed voice rules, including banned vocabulary and the no-em-dash rule, live in docs/brand/voice.md.

## Anti-references

- **Stuffy or dated church sites.** The "too old" read (Romanesque arches, paper-grain texture, oxblood heritage bands) that the 2026-06-14 brand-direction debate deliberately moved away from. See docs/research/2026-06-14-brand-direction-debate.md.
- **Hype-y megachurch marketing.** "Life-changing worship experience," the building as the hero of every page, mission-language fluff. Logistics and substance lead, not slogans.
- **Generic SaaS or startup aesthetics.** Gradient-on-navy, the big-hero-metric template, endless identical icon-and-heading card grids, a small tracked eyebrow above every section.

## Design Principles

1. **Say it plainly, especially about money.** Prices, scholarships, and what a course actually asks of you are stated directly, with no hedging or padding.
2. **Show the thinking, not the credentials.** Earn trust with specific reasoning and real substance (what you will read, how a cohort runs) rather than generic claims of prestige.
3. **Welcome first; assume no insider knowledge.** A layperson with no theology background should feel invited, not tested. Define terms, make room for questions and doubt, keep jargon out.
4. **Structure and standards over age and size.** Founded in 2026, so credibility never leans on heritage, a founding year, or enrollment numbers. It comes from credentialed faculty, in-person cohorts, need-based scholarships, and Westminster grounding.
5. **Sound like a friend, not a brochure.** Warm, conversational, concrete about this specific school, and finished when the point is made.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA is the floor. Contrast is verified against the documented guardrails in design.md (body text at least 4.5:1, large text at least 3:1). Reduced motion is fully respected: a prefers-reduced-motion reset neutralizes the kinetic editorial motion system. Keyboard and focus states are tested on every UI change, and performance is treated as an accessibility concern, with Lighthouse 100/100/100/100 and zero CLS defended. A faculty-editable /accessibility statement page exists with a barrier-report contact. Mobile-first at ~375px is the assumed default.
